<p>Lipavský said that, according to the plan, Ukraine could receive as much as €3 billion this year alone, 90% of which would go directly to the Ukrainian military. “Russia must pay for its war damages,” he said.</p>
<p>The European Commission approved diverting proceeds from Russian assets to Ukraine earlier in the year, and proposed starting payments as early as July, Bloomberg <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-18/eu-drafts-law-to-send-russian-central-bank-profits-to-ukraine?srnd=undefined" rel="noopener" target="_blank" title="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-18/eu-drafts-law-to-send-russian-central-bank-profits-to-ukraine?srnd=undefined">wrote</a> in March.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2022/06/06/could-seizing-russian-assets-help-rebuild-ukraine" rel="noopener" target="_blank" title="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2022/06/06/could-seizing-russian-assets-help-rebuild-ukraine">Data</a> from Russia’s Central Bank for 2022 showed that €289 worth of its foreign assets had been frozen due to sanctions, of which €88 billion were in Germany, €56 billion in France, and €12.8 billion in Austria.</p>
<p>“The proposal to channel Russian assets to Ukraine is a violation of the fundamentals of international law,” Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov <a href="https://t.me/rian_ru/236631" rel="noopener" target="_blank" title="https://t.me/rian_ru/236631">said</a> in March, before threatening the European Commission with legal action. </p>
