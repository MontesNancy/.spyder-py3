style="background-color:#E0DFDE;" class="sc-hpnhKs emUCGX gpt-slot"></div><div id="dw_d_article_top" style="background-color:#E0DFDE;" class="sc-hpnhKs kGlHFU gpt-slot"></div></div><div class="sc-ivDvhZ iBEcrE sc-ilpitK eqflEz sc-jIJgYh fNjber sc-gXCJSa laAjLu sc-bouBDN ihKGDI rich-text"><p><a class="internal-link" href="/en/booker-prize-winner-jenny-erpenbeck-exploring-the-past-from-an-east-german-perspective/a-69099218">German author Jenny Erpenbeck</a> and translator Michael Hofmann on Tuesday won the&nbsp;International Booker Prize for fiction for the book "Kairos."</p>

<p>The book was chosen from 149 submissions for the UK-based award. The prize money of £50,000&nbsp;($64,000, €59,000) will&nbsp;be divided&nbsp;between the translator and the author.</p><blockquote class="instagram embed" data-instagram="true" data-href="https://www.instagram.com/p/C7PvcnSCks6/"></blockquote>

<p><a class="internal-link" href="/en/prophetic-bulgarian-novel-wins-international-booker-prize/a-65724242">Last year's winner was "Time Shelter" by Bulgarian author Georgi Gospodinov</a> and translated by Angela Rodel.</p>

<p>The <a class="internal-link" href="/en/man-booker-prize/t-40013466">Booker Prize for English-language fiction</a> will be handed out in the fall.</p>

<h2>What did the award panel say about Erpenbeck's 'Kairos'?</h2>

<p>"Kairos" follows the story of a young woman's "destructive affair" with an older man in East <a class="internal-link" href="/en/berlin/t-18967759">Berlin</a> in the 1980s.</p>

<p>Canadian broadcaster Eleanor Wachtel, who chaired the five-member panel that chose the winner, called the novel "a richly textured evocation of a tormented love affair, the entanglement of personal and national transformations."</p>

<p>"Like [former East Germany], (the book) starts with optimism and trust, then unravels so badly," she said.</p>

<p>"The self-absorption of the lovers, their descent into a destructive vortex, remains connected to the larger history of East Germany during this period, often meeting history at odd angles."</p>

<p>Wachtel said that Hofmann's translation maintained the "eloquence and eccentricities" of Erpenbeck's writing.</p>

<h2>More to East Germany than the Stasi</h2>

<p>Erpenbeck said she hoped the book would bring to readers aspects of <a class="internal-link" href="/en/east-germany-gdr/t-19035001">former East Germany</a> other than pervasive state surveillance and repression.</p>

<p>"The only thing that everybody knows is that they had a wall, they were terrorizing everyone with the Stasi, and that's it," she said.</p>

<p>"That is not all there is."</p>

<p>The <a class="internal-link" href="/en/stasi/t-38855790">Stasi was the state security authority of communist former East Germany</a>, known as the German Democratic Republic (GDR).</p>

<p>"What interested me is that breaking free is not the only thing that can be told in such a story," she said, referring to the <a class="internal-link" href="/en/berlin-wall/t-17445318">fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989</a>.</p>

<p>"There are years before and years after," she stressed.</p>

<p>Erpenbeck was born and raised in East Berlin, which was part of East Germany&nbsp;until <a class="internal-link" href="/en/german-reunification/t-50703687">German reunification in 1990</a>.</p>
